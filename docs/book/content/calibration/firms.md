# Calibration of Firms Parameters

The [OG-Core firm theory documentation](https://pslmodels.github.io/OG-Core/content/theory/firms.html) describes the constant returns to scale, constant elasticity of substitution (CES) production function used by the representative firm in each of the model's $M$ production industries.

## Aggregate Production Function and Capital Accumulation

The production function for industry $m$ is given as:

```{math}
:label: EqFirmsCESprodfun
  \begin{split}
    Y_{m,t} &= F(K_{m,t}, K_{g,m,t}, L_{m,t}) \\
    &\equiv Z_{m,t}\biggl[(\gamma_m)^\frac{1}{\varepsilon_m}(K_{m,t})^\frac{\varepsilon_m-1}{\varepsilon_m} + (\gamma_{g,m})^\frac{1}{\varepsilon_m}(K_{g,m,t})^\frac{\varepsilon_m-1}{\varepsilon_m} + \\
    &\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad(1-\gamma_m-\gamma_{g,m})^\frac{1}{\varepsilon_m}(e^{g_y t}L_{m,t})^\frac{\varepsilon_m-1}{\varepsilon_m}\biggr]^\frac{\varepsilon_m}{\varepsilon_m-1} \quad\forall m,t
  \end{split}
```

This production function has the following parameters:
* $\varepsilon_m$ is the elasticity of substitution between private capital, government capital, and labor in industry $m$.
* $\gamma_m$ is the share of private capital in industry $m$.
* $\gamma_{g,m}$ is the share of government (infrastructure) capital in industry $m$.
* $Z_{m,t}$ is total factor productivity in industry $m$ at time $t$.

`OG-PHL` ships a single-industry default calibration and an optional eight-industry (`M=8`) calibration. The single-industry parameters are described immediately below; the multi-industry calibration -- its industries, the per-industry capital shares $\gamma_m$, the input-output matrix, and the sector productivities $Z_m$ -- is described in the sections that follow.

### Elasticity of substitution

`OG-PHL`'s default parameterization has an elasticity of substitution of $\varepsilon=1.0$, which implies a Cobb-Douglas production function. No Philippine estimate of the capital-labor substitution elasticity is available, and $\varepsilon=1$ is the OG-Core default and the value used by the sibling country models.

### Factor shares of output

Philippine national accounts data suggest that the capital share of income is 58.785%. Of that, we allocate 5 percentage points to infrastructure (government) capital, $\gamma_{g,m}=0.05$ for all $m$, leaving private capital's share at $\gamma_m=0.53785$. We are simply allocating 5% of the capital share of output to infrastructure investment. We tried a higher percentage of 10%, but the steady-state solution would not solve.

## The Multi-Industry Calibration

`OG-PHL` includes an eight-industry ($M=8$, $I=5$ consumption goods) calibration built from the packaged 2018 IFPRI Social Accounting Matrix (SAM) and Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) data. Because (re-)calibration is a rare event, the calibration is stored as a static parameter overlay, `ogphl_multisector_default_parameters.json`, which the model loads rather than recomputing at run time. The overlay contains only the parameters the multi-industry calibration changes (`M`, `I`, `alpha_c`, `io_matrix`, `c_min`, the per-industry `gamma`, `epsilon`, `gamma_g`, `Z`, the units-converted `chi_b`/`chi_n`, the dampening `nu`, and the `TPI_outer_method` solver choice); it is applied on top of the single-industry base `ogphl_default_parameters.json`, so everything economy-wide — demographics, earnings, tax functions, the fiscal and open-economy blocks — is inherited from the base at load time and a base recalibration flows into the multi-industry model automatically. (The overlay is not a standalone calibration: loaded alone, the omitted parameters would fall back to OG-Core's US-calibrated defaults.) The file is (re)generated by the tool `ogphl.create_multisector_calibration`, which assembles the construction functions in `ogphl.input_output` (`get_alpha_c`, `get_io_matrix_value_added`, `get_gamma`, `get_Z`, `get_employment`). The overlay is a build artifact — every value in it is computed, and a test keeps the committed file identical to the builder's output — so it is never edited by hand. The calibration's editable inputs are the packaged SAM and Labor Force Survey extracts in `ogphl/data/` and the aggregation and factor-share constants in `ogphl.constants` (`PROD_DICT`, `CONS_DICT`, `TOTAL_CAPITAL_SHARE`, `PUBLIC_CAPITAL_SHARE`): changing the calibration means editing one of those and rerunning the builder. One-off experiments (a different tax rate, a solver setting) belong in a run script via `update_specifications`, layered on top of the packaged files at load time, exactly as the example's reform scenario does. The example `examples/run_og_phl_multi_industry_calibrated.py` loads the base then the overlay, solves the steady state, and runs a baseline and a trivial reform scenario.

### Industries

The eight industries, in the order the model uses them, are:

1. Agriculture & Fishing
2. Mining
3. Electricity
4. Water
5. Construction
6. Trade & Transport
7. Services
8. Manufacturing

Manufacturing is kept **last** because OG-Core treats the final industry $M$ as the numeraire (all prices are relative to it) and the sole producer of investment goods. The former utilities sector is split into separate Electricity and Water industries. The industries are aggregations of the SAM's production activities; see `ogphl.constants.PROD_DICT` for the exact mapping.

### Private capital's share by industry

The per-industry capital shares $\gamma_m$ are computed from the factor-payment rows of the SAM as

$$\gamma_m = \frac{\text{capital income}_m}{\text{labor income}_m + \text{capital income}_m},$$

where capital income is the sum of payments to capital and land (OG-Core has only two private factors, so land's return is grouped with capital). These raw shares are *total* capital shares -- the SAM has no separate public-capital row -- and they come out too high because it records the earnings of self-employed owner-operators (large in Philippine agriculture and trade) as a return to capital rather than splitting out their labor income. We therefore rescale every industry's total capital share by a common factor so that, weighted by industry size, they average to the economy-wide capital share of 0.588 (from the ILOSTAT labor share, an independent source), then subtract public capital's share $\gamma_g=0.05$ to leave the private capital share $\gamma_m$. Carving $\gamma_g$ out of capital rather than labor keeps the economy-wide labor share at 0.412, matching the national accounts; the rescaling fixes the *level* while preserving the cross-industry *pattern* (Mining and Electricity capital-intensive, Services and Agriculture labor-intensive). This is implemented in `ogphl.input_output.get_gamma` (the rescale) and `ogphl.create_multisector_calibration` (the $\gamma_g$ split). The resulting shares are in the table below.

Public capital's share is held at $\gamma_{g,m}=0.05$ for every industry, the single-industry value (the model carries a positive public capital stock, `initial_Kg_ratio=0.2`, so the same infrastructure role applies to every industry).

### The input-output matrix

OG-Core maps household demand for the $I$ consumption goods into demand for the $M$ industries' output through the input-output matrix `io_matrix` (the $\pi_{i,m}$ coefficients in the [OG-Core market-clearing documentation](https://pslmodels.github.io/OG-Core/content/theory/market_clearing.html)). Because OG-Core's production side has no intermediate inputs (each industry's gross output equals its value added), the object needed is the **value added embodied in final demand**: of one peso spent on consumption good $i$, how much value added accrues to each industry $m$ once the full domestic supply chain is traced.

`ogphl.input_output.get_io_matrix_value_added` builds this from the SAM as a standard input-output (Leontief inverse) value-added-content calculation, with imported content netted out and each row renormalized to sum to one. This correctly attributes consumption to producing industries -- for example, household energy spending maps to roughly 74% electricity rather than the ~54% manufacturing that a naive direct-use matrix implies.

### Sector total factor productivity

In the single-industry model we normalize $Z_{m,t}=1.0$. For the multi-industry model, `OG-ZAF` sets sector TFP from the South African estimates in {cite}`PRS2020`. No comparable published series of sector TFP *levels* exists for the Philippines, so `OG-PHL` constructs $Z_m$ directly as the Solow residual {cite}`Solow:1956` of OG-Core's own technology,

```{math}
:label: EqZmSolow
  Z_m = \frac{Y_m}{K_m^{\gamma_m}\,K_{g}^{\gamma_{g,m}}\,L_m^{1-\gamma_m-\gamma_{g,m}}},
```

normalized so the numeraire industry (Manufacturing) has $Z_m=1.0$. Public capital $K_g$ is one aggregate stock shared by every industry, so the $K_g^{\gamma_{g,m}}$ term is common across industries and cancels under the normalization; in practice only $\gamma_{g,m}$'s effect on the labor exponent enters, and $K_g$ itself is never needed. The base year is 2018. The inputs are:

* $Y_m$ -- industry value added, from the 2018 SAM (the same totals that define $\gamma_m$).
* $L_m$ -- industry employment, from the [PSA Labor Force Survey](https://psa.gov.ph/statistics/labor-force-survey). Employment is the one input measured *independently* of the SAM, which is what keeps $Z_m$ from collapsing into a mechanical function of the factor shares.
* $K_m$ -- industry capital, allocated from the national capital stock by each industry's share of capital income. Because OG-Core's capital is mobile across industries at a common net return, the equilibrium capital distribution is proportional to capital income, so this allocation is model-consistent and gives full-economy coverage (informal and imputed capital). The national capital stock is anchored to the Penn World Table {cite}`FeenstraEtAl:2015` capital-output ratio for the Philippines.

This follows the perpetual-inventory tradition of the Philippine Institute for Development Studies (PIDS) sectoral-TFP work {cite}`CororatonCuenca:2001`. We prefer the capital-income allocation to PIDS's original establishment-survey "gross additions to fixed assets" (GAFA) route, which covers only formal firms and so omits informal capital and inverts the productivity ranking; the result is cross-checked against the GGDC/UNU-WIDER Economic Transformation Database {cite}`deVriesEtAl:2021`. See `ogphl.input_output.get_Z` for the comparison and robustness checks.

The resulting capital shares and sector productivities are:

| Industry | $\gamma_m$ | $Z_m$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Agriculture & Fishing | 0.464 | 0.559 |
| Mining | 0.785 | 0.579 |
| Electricity | 0.782 | 0.854 |
| Water | 0.684 | 0.907 |
| Construction | 0.586 | 0.727 |
| Trade & Transport | 0.549 | 0.783 |
| Services | 0.473 | 1.081 |
| Manufacturing | 0.624 | 1.000 |

Agriculture is the least productive industry and Services and the utilities the most -- consistent with the structure of the Philippine economy (low-productivity smallholder agriculture; a large, relatively productive services sector; capital-intensive, high-value-added utilities).

### Converting the utility weights to the multi-good consumption units

OG-Core prices the composite consumption bundle by dividing each good's tax-inclusive price by its budget share before taking the share-weighted (geometric) average: $\tilde{p} = \prod_i \left((1+\tau_i)p_i/\alpha_i\right)^{\alpha_i}$ (`aggregates.get_ptilde`). With one consumption good the share is 1 and the division does nothing; with several goods the shares are fractions, and dividing by fractions inflates the index by a constant $k = \prod_i \alpha_i^{-\alpha_i}$ that depends only on the budget shares. A two-good toy makes it visible: two goods, each costing 1, each taking half the budget -- each price becomes $1 \div \tfrac{1}{2} = 2$, and the average of 2 and 2 prices the bundle at 2, twice the price of anything in it. The five Philippine budget shares give $k = 2.97$. Nothing real changes: the same basket carries a price about 3x higher and a measured quantity about 3x smaller than in the single-good model.

Two preference parameters are defined relative to consumption and get caught by that units change: $\chi_n$ sets how painful an hour of work is compared with the consumption it buys, and $\chi_b$ sets how much a bequest is worth compared with consuming the same resources. Both are plain numbers calibrated in the single-good units, so they do not move when the units do. After the remeasurement a peso of consumption looks $k^{\sigma-1} \approx 1.72\times$ more valuable in utility terms while the two weights stay put, and every work and bequest tradeoff tips toward consumption: unconverted, the same preferences deliver more labor and less saving, which raises the domestic return by roughly 180 basis points and doubles the foreign-owned capital share relative to the single-industry baseline.

The multi-industry calibration therefore scales both weights by $k^{\sigma-1}$ ($= 2.97^{0.5} \approx 1.723$ at $\sigma = 1.5$). The factor is derived from the budget shares and the utility curvature, not fitted, and it restores the household first-order conditions exactly. `ogphl.create_multisector_calibration` computes it from `alpha_c`, so it regenerates whenever the consumption shares do, and a test pins the relationship.

## Solving the Multi-Industry Steady State

With heterogeneous capital shares and TFP across industries, the equilibrium relative prices $p_m$ are far from one (they range from about 0.4 to 3.3). But OG-Core seeds every industry-price guess at $p_m=1$ and its built-in guess search varies only the interest rate $r$ and transfers $TR$ -- never $p_m$. A direct solve from that seed therefore diverges.

`OG-PHL` instead solves by *continuation* (homotopy), in `solve_ss_by_continuation`:

1. Solve a **homogeneous anchor economy** in which every industry has the flat capital share $\gamma=0.53785$ and $Z=1$. In that economy all equilibrium prices genuinely equal one, matching OG-Core's seed, so it solves on the first try. The anchor is economically the same aggregate economy as the single-industry model, and it cold-starts from the base JSON's steady-state seeds (`initial_guess_r_SS`, `initial_guess_TR_SS`, `initial_guess_factor_SS`), which are retuned whenever the shared economy is recalibrated.
2. Introduce a dial $t\in[0,1]$ that blends the anchor toward the calibrated economy: $\gamma(t) = (1-t)\,\gamma_{\text{flat}} + t\,\gamma_m$ and $Z(t) = (1-t)\cdot 1 + t\,Z_m$. Both the capital shares and TFP are morphed *together*; if $Z$ were left heterogeneous at the anchor, prices would not start at one and the first step would fail.
3. Walk $t$ from 0 to 1 in adaptive steps. Each step is solved as a reform that re-uses the previous step's solution as its starting guess (this is how the previous step's prices are carried forward, since OG-Core does not accept a price guess directly). The step size grows after a success and halves after a failure, because the capital-intensive industries and the TFP dispersion move prices enough that a fixed step can overshoot.

At $t=1$ the blended parameters are the calibrated parameters, so the converged $t=1$ solution **is** the calibrated steady state.

Because the calibrated steady state cannot be re-solved from a cold start, the example's baseline transition path *reuses* this converged steady state (it places the converged steady state and runs only the time path), rather than re-solving it; the reform scenario re-solves its own steady state warm-started off the baseline, which converges because it is only a small policy perturbation.

The transition path needs heavier damping than the OG-Core default. Of the values we tested, $\nu=0.4$ (the default) locks into a constant-amplitude oscillation; $\nu=0.3$ converges but with little stability margin and is barely faster overall (runtime is dominated by the continuation anchor's cold steady-state solve, which $\nu$ does not affect); $\nu=0.2$ converges robustly, so the calibration uses it. The data-anchored $\zeta_K=0.4$ also matters here — it gives domestic capital the buffer the path needs to clear (see {ref}`Chap_MacroCalib`).

## Interpreting the Multi-Industry Steady State

Several features of the converged steady state are structural properties of OG-Core's open-economy, single-numeraire accounting, **not** artifacts of the calibration (a control run with $Z_m=1$ for all $m$ reproduces them):

* **Manufacturing's nominal output share (~65%) is mechanically inflated.** OG-Core routes all non-consumption final demand -- investment, government spending, net capital outflows -- through the single numeraire industry, so its nominal share far exceeds Manufacturing's ~19% value-added share in the data. The data-comparable object is the value-added/consumption composition, which tracks the SAM for the large sectors (Services ~38-40%) while understating Trade & Transport.
* **The foreign-owned capital share is set by the open-economy parameters** ({ref}`Chap_MacroCalib`) and the consumption-units conversion above, not the industry split. With the data-anchored $\zeta_K=0.4$, the 5% world rate, and the converted utility weights, the foreign share is ~34% of the stock (against ~26% in the single-industry baseline and ~20% in the BSP IIP; the old $\zeta_K=0.9$ implied ~96%), and the domestic return is ~8.1% against the baseline's 7.1%. The remaining gaps to the baseline are composition effects of the multi-good basket.
* **Consumption is about half of GDP** valued at the composite consumption price ($\tilde{p} \cdot C/Y \approx 0.50$), in line with the single-industry baseline's 0.51. Raw $C/Y$ is not comparable across the two models because composite units differ (see the units conversion above).
